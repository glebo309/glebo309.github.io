Whole-cell biocatalysts by design (Lin & Tao, Microbial Cell Factories 2017)

Citation & scope
Review of strategies to design and optimize whole-cell biocatalysts, with emphasis on E. coli chassis, covering pathway construction, metabolic/chassis engineering, cofactor strategies, transport/mass-transfer, and process engineering.

Thesis / what’s new
Whole-cell systems are increasingly rationally designed (synthetic biology + metabolic engineering) to convert cheap feedstocks into value-added chemicals; success hinges on maximizing pathway flux, balancing cofactors/precursors, and engineering the chassis as much as the enzymes.

Higher-order topic map (as framed by the review)

  1. Why whole cells: inherent cofactor supply, multistep cascades, cost advantages; key drawbacks are membrane mass-transfer limits and side metabolism.

  2. Pathway construction: single-step, multistep (heterologous), and de-novo synthetic pathways; separate growth vs. production phases.

  3. Pathway optimization: identify bottlenecks; balance expression (promoters/RBS/codon/gene order); block competing pathways; boost precursor supply.

  4. Cofactor & redox engineering: classical NAD(P)H recycling (FDH/GDH etc.); self-sufficient redox cascades; 2-oxoglutarate reuse via a modified TCA cycle; “bridging” designs to regenerate multiple cofactors simultaneously.

  5. Chassis engineering: uptake/efflux tuning (e.g., AlkL), reduce product/substrate degradation, mitigate proteolysis.

  6. Process engineering: biomass formation cost vs. activity; permeabilization (surfactants/solvents, temperature shock); two-phase systems.

  7. Control strategies: dynamic regulation to decouple growth and production.

Key exemplars & metrics

  • “Natural” label economics: 2-phenylethanol by bioprocess 20.4–26.5 g/L (≈0.3 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹) vs. much cheaper petro-route product label; price premium drives viability.

  • Single-step: esterase EstK1 in E. coli → cinnamyl acetate; 94–97% conversion in 1–2 h.

  • Single-step (GABA): overexpressed GAD with host deletions → 614 g/L GABA, >99% molar yield.

  • Multistep (2-PE): Ehrlich or PAAS pathways transplanted to E. coli; de-novo PLP-only route produced 0.34 g/L.

  • De-novo Neu5Ac: AGE + NanA (or NeuB) in E. coli → up to 74.2 g/L Neu5Ac; 6.2 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹; recyclable ≥5 cycles. Knocking out nanTEK boosted titers to 173.8 mM.

  • Combinatorial pathway tuning (OLMA): lycopene pathway library raised yield from 0.14 → 15.17 mg/g DCW.

  • Cofactor strategies: classic FDH/GDH NAD(P)H recycling; self-sufficient redox amination (ADH + transaminase + AlaDH); in-vitro hydrogen-borrowing ADH+AmDH cascade.

  • 2-OG as “cofactor”: re-wired TCA to force flux through DAOCS → 11-fold efficiency boost for penicillin-G → 7-ADCA.

  • Transport engineering: AlkL improves uptake for ω-oxyfunctionalization; overexpression can toxify—tune expression to jump from 4.3 → 229 g/L in two-phase setups.

  • Permeabilization: Triton X-100, Tween-80, xylene, CTAB—useful but trade-offs with leakage/downstream burden.

Limits / gaps (authors’ view)
Mass-transfer barriers; side metabolism and product/substrate degradation; rigid gene over/under-expression in central metabolism harms growth; need dynamic control to toggle growth vs. production and avoid toxic intermediates.

Outlook
Rationally designed whole-cell catalysts will expand via combinatorial DNA assembly, systems-level flux control, self-sufficient cofactor networks, and dynamic gene circuits—with E. coli remaining a prime chassis but concepts transferable to other hosts.
