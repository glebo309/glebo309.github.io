Industrial biocatalysis today and tomorrow (Schmid, Dordick, Hauer, Kiener, Wubbolts, Witholt, Nature 2001)

Citation & scope
Industry-focused snapshot of what was already working at scale (BASF, DSM, Lonza) and a pragmatic framework—“the biocatalysis cycle” (from biocatalyst selection → process & DSP → economics)—for taking a reaction to plant. Covers isolated enzymes vs whole cells, non-aqueous & multiphase media, gas-phase biocatalysis, in-situ product recovery, and cofactor recycling (in vivo & in vitro).

Thesis / what’s new
Industrial adoption hinges less on “can enzymes do it?” and more on availability of the right biocatalyst, hardware/DS choices kept simple, and economics; with directed evolution + medium/immobilization engineering, biocatalysis competes across fine chems, APIs, and some bulk.

Higher-order topic map (authors’ framing)

  1. The biocatalysis cycle — iterate across enzyme/cell choice, stability, cofactor regeneration, multiphase media, immobilization, in-situ recovery, and costing.

  2. Media engineering — organic solvents (incl. neat & scCO₂), two-liquid-phase systems for apolar/toxic substrates, and gas-phase biocatalysis; manage toxicity & mass transfer.

  3. Formats — isolated enzymes for hydrolysis/isomerization; whole cells where cofactors are needed (cheaper recycling, multistep). Immobilization standard for reuse.

  4. Cofactor strategies — in vivo (cells) and in vitro (FDH/GDH; CLEC-based LDH; electrochemical & mediator systems; new ATP regeneration with polyphosphate).

  5. Downstream & ISR — precipitation, crystallization, liquid/solid extraction; phase-split from organic carriers in 2-phase systems; align DS with reactor choice early.

  6. Industrial case blocks — BASF (lipase resolutions of alcohols/amines; nitrilase → R-mandelic acid), DSM (amidases → non-proteinogenic L-AAs; aspartame; 6-APA/7-ADCA routes), Lonza (N-heterocycles: ring hydroxylations, nitrilase + hydroxylase sequences; nicotine valorization).

Key exemplars & metrics

  • Whole-cell two-phase oxidations: typical activities 100–500 IU·L⁻¹, sustained 10–20 h, implying 5–25 t·m⁻³·yr⁻¹ STY for small molecules.

  • Cost model (n-alkane → alcohol, 10 000 t·yr⁻¹): ~US8/kg∗∗product;majorcosts:media(carbon/salts) ∗∗4020–30/kg (improvements might reach $5–10/kg).

  • BASF lipase platforms: chiral alcohols & amines (vinyl/activated donors) at hundreds-ton scale; nitrilase to R-mandelic acid at multiton.

  • DSM: amidases resolve Strecker amide libraries to L-AAs; thermolysin couples Z-Asp + D/L-Phe-OMe → aspartame; penicillin acylase for 6-APA and side-chain couplings.

  • Lonza: 6-hydroxynicotinic acid 65 g·L⁻¹, ~90% yield; 5-hydroxypyrazinecarboxylic acid 40 g·L⁻¹; (S)-nicotine → value-added pyridines (up to 30 g·L⁻¹).

  • In-vitro cofactor: Degussa L-tert-leucine with FDH/NADH recycling; HLADH-CLEC packed bed: NADH TTN ≈ 12 000; electrochemical NAD(P)H regeneration demonstrated.

Limits / gaps (authors’ view)
Volumetric O₂ input/heat removal constrain whole-cell oxygenations at scale; solvent toxicity & mass transfer need 2-phase engineering; biocatalyst availability is often the real gate; regulatory & waste handling still shape choices.

Outlook
Expect growth wherever selectivity + DS simplicity pay: lipases, nitrilases, amidases remain mainstays; multiphase/gas-phase and in-situ recovery broaden the window; ongoing evolution/engineering and cofactor tech widen the addressable product value range.

Biocatalysis Field Topics - From Schmid et al. Nature 2001

Document ID: Schmid_2001_Nature

Core Topics Extracted:

1. The Biocatalysis Development Cycle

  • Definition: Systematic approach to developing industrial biocatalytic processes from product identification to economic implementation
  • Components: Biocatalyst selection, process engineering, economic evaluation, and iterative optimization
  • Application: Framework for rational development of commercial bioprocesses

2. Economic Feasibility Analysis

  • Definition: Cost analysis methodology for determining commercial viability of biocatalytic processes
  • Benchmark: Products valued >$20-30/kg with >1000 tons/year production and >100 IU/L volumetric activity
  • Cost Distribution: Equipment (18-22%), substrates (20%), medium/glucose (40%), energy (10%), waste (5%), personnel (2%)

3. Two-Liquid Phase Biocatalysis

  • Definition: Biotransformation systems using aqueous phase for cells and organic phase for substrates/products
  • Advantages: Handles toxic/hydrophobic compounds, enables efficient product recovery, uses established downstream processing
  • Applications: Alkane oxidation, aromatic compound transformation, heterocycle modification

4. Enzyme Catalysis in Organic Solvents

  • Definition: Biocatalytic reactions performed in non-aqueous media to overcome water solubility limitations
  • Benefits: Higher substrate solubility, reversed hydrolytic reactions, modified enzyme specificity
  • Activation Methods: Salt activation (>20,000-fold enhancement), crown ethers, transition-state analogues

5. Salt-Activated Enzyme Systems

  • Definition: Dramatic enzyme activation achieved through lyophilization with kosmotropic salts
  • Mechanism: Salts stabilize folded enzyme forms during lyophilization and in organic solvents
  • Performance: >20,000-fold rate enhancements with subtilisin and other enzymes in organic media

6. Biocatalytic Plastics Technology

  • Definition: Incorporation of enzymes into polymeric materials during polymerization to create immobilized biocatalysts
  • Applications: Packed-bed reactors, biocatalytic coatings, antifouling surfaces, diagnostic materials
  • Advantages: Controlled particle sizes, tailored material properties, enhanced stability

7. In Situ Product Recovery (ISPR)

  • Definition: Simultaneous product separation during biotransformation to overcome inhibition and improve yields
  • Methods: Crystallization, distillation, liquid extraction, adsorption onto solid phases
  • Benefits: Overcomes substrate/product inhibition, prevents product decay, enables higher concentrations

8. Industrial Lipase Applications

  • Definition: Commercial use of lipases for enantioselective transformations in pharmaceutical and chemical industries
  • Mechanisms: Transesterification with vinyl esters/anhydrides for kinetic resolution of alcohols and amines
  • Scale: BASF produces hundreds of tons annually of chiral alcohols and amines using lipase processes

9. Nitrilase-Based Industrial Processes

  • Definition: Commercial production of amino- and hydroxycarboxylic acids through stereoselective nitrile hydrolysis
  • Strategy: Integration with existing HCN chemistry to access new chiral intermediates
  • Example: BASF’s multiton-scale R-mandelic acid production from racemizing cyanohydrins

10. Non-Proteinogenic Amino Acid Production

  • Definition: Industrial synthesis of unnatural amino acids through enzymatic resolution of racemic amides
  • Technology: DSM’s amidase toolbox from P. putida, M. neoaurum, O. antropii with varied substrate ranges
  • Recycling: Both enantiomers accessible through racemization of undesired isomer

11. Semisynthetic Antibiotic Manufacturing

  • Definition: Green biocatalytic routes for producing β-lactam antibiotics replacing harsh chemical processes
  • Process: Penicillin acylase for 6-APA production, enzymatic coupling for semisynthetic derivatives
  • Advantages: Mild conditions preserving labile β-lactam rings, eliminating low-temperature chemistry

12. N-Heterocycle Functionalization

  • Definition: Lonza’s biocatalytic routes for selective modification of aromatic N-heterocycles
  • Applications: Selective hydroxylation, kinetic resolution, oxidation of methyl groups
  • Examples: 6-hydroxynicotinic acid (65 g/L), 5-hydroxypyrazinecarboxylic acid for antituberculosis drugs

13. Renewable Resource Utilization

  • Definition: Using natural materials like nicotine from tobacco for pharmaceutical precursor synthesis
  • Strategy: Selective functionalization of natural alkaloids to produce drug analogues
  • Products: Epibatidine analogues, functionalized pyridines from (S)-nicotine biotransformation

14. Whole-Cell Cofactor Regeneration

  • Definition: Using metabolically active cells for in vivo regeneration of expensive cofactors like NAD(P)H
  • Advantages: Eliminates need for external cofactor addition, enables complex multi-enzyme reactions
  • Applications: Oxidoreductions, phosphorylated compound synthesis, oligosaccharide production

15. Steroid Biotransformation Industry

  • Definition: Large-scale conversion of plant sterols to pharmaceutical precursors using microbial systems
  • Scale: >1000 tons/year worldwide market for androstenedione and androsta-dien-dione
  • Processes: Hydroxylations (11α/11β), dehydrogenations, reductions using fungal and bacterial strains

16. Electrochemical Cofactor Regeneration

  • Definition: In vitro NAD(P)H regeneration using electrical current and mediator compounds
  • Methods: Rhodium complexes, mediator proteins, artificial redox mediators
  • Applications: Enables isolated enzyme use for oxidoreduction reactions without expensive cofactor consumption

17. Gas-Phase Biocatalysis

  • Definition: Enzyme or cell catalysis with gaseous reactants, avoiding toxic solvents
  • Advantages: Simple downstream processing, efficient mass transfer, avoids solvent toxicity
  • Suitability: Compounds that can be volatilized at operating conditions

18. High-Density Cell Cultivation

  • Definition: Achieving 10-40 g/L dry cell mass in two-phase systems for industrial biotransformations
  • Performance: 100-500 IU/L volumetric activities, 10-20 hour stability, 5-25 tons/m³/year space-time yields
  • Host: E. coli preferred for recombinant systems despite limited solvent resistance

19. Combinatorial Biocatalysis

  • Definition: High-throughput generation of compound libraries using enzymatic transformations
  • Applications: Drug discovery, agrochemicals, biopolymers, nutraceuticals, separation media
  • Advantages: Enzyme selectivity enables diverse natural product and synthetic derivatives

20. Future Biocatalytic Technologies

  • Definition: Emerging technologies combining miniaturization, automation, and advanced enzyme engineering
  • Innovations: Biochips, microfluidic systems, protein-based nanomaterials, directed evolution integration
  • Potential: Proteomics applications, functional genomics, automated library screening

Review Character: Comprehensive industrial perspective covering current commercial processes and future technological directions from leading European chemical companies.

Unique Contributions: Real-world economic analysis, detailed industrial case studies, concrete production scales and costs, technology transfer insights from academic research to industrial implementation.

Key Insights: Demonstrates that biocatalysis has moved beyond proof-of-concept to large-scale commercial reality, with clear economic drivers and technological enablers for continued growth.
