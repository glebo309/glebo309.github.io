Engineering the third wave of biocatalysis (Bornscheuer et al., Nature 2012)

Citation & scope
Perspective/review that coins and frames the “third wave” of biocatalysis: directed evolution + DNA tech + bioinformatics turning bespoke enzymes into robust, process-fit catalysts; surveys enabling methods and industrial case studies (Lipitor side chain, sitagliptin, others).

Thesis / what’s new
Advances in sequencing/synthesis, smarter evolution, and computational analysis allow large, rapid improvements in stability, activity, and selectivity, so enzymes are now engineered to fit process specs (not vice-versa). The article formalizes a free-energy/ΔG design view that links process goals to structural changes and library strategies.

Higher-order topic map (authors’ framing)

  1. Three waves — 1st: immobilized/stable natural enzymes for classic products; 2nd: early structure-based engineering expanding scope; 3rd: directed evolution with smarter, smaller libraries and rapid DNA build/read.

  2. ΔG-guided engineering — Map target property gains (stability/selectivity/rate) to free-energy changes, pick strategies (e.g., surface ion pairs, prolines, reshaped pockets), then deploy focused libraries near causal regions. (Fig. 2).

  3. DNA & informatics stack — Cheap gene synthesis (whole-gene/library), massive NGS/metagenomes, consensus/ancestral analyses, structure databases → small, high-quality libraries with high active-variant fraction.

  4. Library & screening tactics — Random ↔ semirational (active-site saturation; reduced alphabets), ProSAR stats to combine beneficial mutations; FACS/IVC, growth-based selections, and continuous evolution in E. coli.

  5. From whole cells to isolated enzymes — Trend toward isolated enzymes (more active per mass, easier DS, harsher media tolerance), with whole-cell exceptions when cofactor recycling and multienzyme logic dominate.

  6. Process & sustainability — Numerous Presidential Green Chemistry Awards to biocatalysis; case-based evidence that evolved enzymes cut waste, metals, energy and simplify work-ups at scale.

Key exemplars & metrics (table-ready)

  • Sitagliptin TA: 27 mutations (8.2% of 330 aa); converts 200 g/L ketone to product, 99.95% ee, 92% yield; +53% productivity & metal-free vs Rh/Josiphos route.

  • Lipitor side chain (Fig. 3): alternative KRED+HHDH, nitrilase (handles up to 3 M substrate; 96% conv., 98.5% ee), or aldolase route (sets two stereocenters); one KRED/HHDH intermediate at >100 t·yr⁻¹ rate.

  • ProSAR-guided HHDH: ~4,000× rate gain with 35 substitutions (14% of 254 aa) identified statistically across big random libraries.

  • Scale portfolio (Table): many KRED/TA/oxidase/hydrolase processes (e.g., ezetimibe, boceprevir/telaprevir intermediates, simvastatin acylation) enabled by stability, solvent tolerance, product inhibition relief.

  • Green awards: 16 biocatalysis wins since 2000 (e.g., sitagliptin, atorvastatin intermediate, PLA, 1,3-PDO, biodiesel).

Limits / gaps (authors’ view)

  • Design predictability: distal mutation effects & non-additivity/epistasis remain hard; need better models of dynamics and transition states.

  • De-novo design: successful but weak starters that still require evolution (Kemp, Diels–Alder).

  • Practical constraints: DNA synthesis cost/length; thermodynamic ceilings for some non-natural transformations; structure under process conditions vs crystal conditions may diverge.

Outlook (authors)
Expect more 30–40-mutation catalysts routinely tailored in months, broader promiscuity-to-new-function upgrades, tighter nanomaterial/scaffold integration, and continued shift to process-fit enzyme design as a standard part of synthesis.

Biocatalysis Field Topics - Extracted from Key Review

Source: Bornscheuer, U.T., Huisman, G.W., Kazlauskas, R.J., Lutz, S., Moore, J.C. & Robins, K. “Engineering the third wave of biocatalysis.” Nature 485, 185-194 (2012).

Core Topics Identified

1. Directed Evolution

Definition: Iterative cycles of random amino-acid changes in proteins, followed by selection or screening for improved variants. Key Quote: “The methods are now commonly called directed evolution… The initial versions of this technology involve iterative cycles of random amino-acid changes in a protein, followed by selection or screening of the resulting libraries for variants with improved enzyme stability, substrate specificity and enantioselectivity.”

2. Protein Engineering Strategies

Definition: Rational, semi-rational, and computational approaches to enzyme improvement. Key Quote: “Converting these case studies into engineering principles will require using free energy to connect the design goals to the structural changes needed… Large changes in properties require large changes in free energy.”

3. Industrial Pharmaceutical Biocatalysis

Definition: Commercial-scale enzymatic processes for drug intermediate synthesis. Key Quote: “The numerous biocatalytic routes scaled up for pharmaceutical manufacturing demonstrate their competitiveness with traditional chemical processes.” Examples: Atorvastatin (Lipitor), sitagliptin, duloxetine synthesis

4. Ketoreductases (KREDs)

Definition: Enzymes for stereoselective reduction of ketones to alcohols. Key Quote: “KRED-based processes have now replaced whole-cell reductions and metal–ligand-based chemocatalysis, which were the industry standards during the past decade.”

5. Transaminases

Definition: Enzymes for stereoselective synthesis of chiral amines. Key Quote: “Recent engineering expanded the substrate range of transaminases to ketones with two bulky substituents… yielded an engineered amine transaminase that can replace the transition-metal-based hydrogenation catalyst for sitagliptin manufacture.”

6. Cofactor Regeneration

Definition: Systems for continuous recycling of expensive cofactors (NADH, NADPH). Key Quote: “As predicted by Schmid et al. in their forward-looking review in 2001, continuous regeneration of cofactors and a wider range of enzymes have been reported in the past ten years.”

7. Stability Engineering

Definition: Improving enzyme thermostability, solvent tolerance, and operational stability. Key Quote: “Engineered enzymes can remain stable at 60°C in solutions containing organic solvents, can accept new substrates and can catalyse new non-natural reactions.”

8. Substrate Scope Expansion

Definition: Engineering enzymes to accept non-natural substrates. Key Quote: “Novel enzymes are needed today to convert biomass into second- and third-generation biofuels, materials and chemicals.”

9. Green Chemistry Applications

Definition: Environmentally friendly biocatalytic processes. Key Quote: “Biocatalysis, using either enzyme technology or whole cells, has won 16 awards since 2000… Biocatalysts are made from renewable sources and are biodegradable and non-toxic.”

10. Multi-enzyme Systems & Cascades

Definition: Sequential enzymatic reactions for complex molecule synthesis. Key Quote: “The single-enzyme cascade reactions as catalysed by aldolases have been further expanded to multi-enzyme cascade processes, for instance in the synthesis of 2’-deoxyinosine in vitro or of complex molecules such as artemisinin or Taxol in vivo.”

11. Bioinformatics & Sequence Analysis

Definition: Computational tools for enzyme discovery and engineering. Key Quote: “Bioinformatics tools have become an integral part of modern protein engineering. Multiple sequence alignments across large enzyme families and homology searches have identified genes with similar catalytic activities.”

12. DNA Technologies

Definition: Gene synthesis, mutagenesis, and molecular cloning techniques. Key Quote: “Low-cost DNA synthesis has replaced isolation of genomic DNA as the starting point for protein engineering… Customized genes and even gene libraries are becoming commodity chemicals.”

13. Metabolic Engineering

Definition: Redesigning cellular pathways for biocatalytic production. Key Quote: “The method of disabling unwanted pathways to increase flux to the desired product is further developed by the third advance, metabolic pathway engineering.”

14. Computational Enzyme Design

Definition: Ab initio design of new enzymatic activities. Key Quote: “Computer design of new enzyme activities is not accurate. Design still requires testing 10–20 predictions and usually results in an enzyme with low activity.”

15. Process Integration

Definition: Incorporating biocatalysts into manufacturing processes. Key Quote: “In the past, an enzyme-based process was designed around the limitations of the enzyme; today, the enzyme is engineered to fit the process specifications.”

Historical Context (Three Waves)

First Wave (1900s+): Basic enzyme applications

  • Example: “Rosenthaler synthesized (R)-mandelonitrile from benzaldehyde and hydrogen cyanide using a plant extract”

Second Wave (1980s-1990s): Structure-based protein engineering

  • Example: “lipase-catalysed resolution of chiral precursors for synthesis of diltiazem”

Third Wave (1990s+): Directed evolution and advanced engineering

  • Example: “They pioneered molecular biology methods that rapidly and extensively modify biocatalysts via an in vitro version of Darwinian evolution”

Key Quantitative Achievements

  • Sitagliptin transaminase: 40,000-fold activity increase
  • Halohydrin dehalogenase: >4,000-fold rate improvement with 35 amino acid changes
  • Modern engineering: 30-40 amino acid substitutions typical (vs. 1-5 in early 2000s)
  • DNA sequencing cost: Decreased 1,000-fold from 70Mto<10K (2002-2012)
