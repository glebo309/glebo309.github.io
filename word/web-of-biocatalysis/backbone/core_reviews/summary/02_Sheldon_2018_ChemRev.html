Role of Biocatalysis in Sustainable Chemistry (Sheldon & Woodley, Chem. Rev. 2018)

Citation & scope
Programmatic review connecting green metrics and process design to practical biocatalysis: media (water/organic/scCO₂/ILs/DESs), biocatalysis engineering (protein, substrate, immobilization, reactor/ISR/flow), cascades, and bio-based economy use-cases.

Thesis / what’s new
Biocatalysis is intrinsically green & sustainable when engineered “by design”: pair directed evolution with medium/immobilization/reactor choices, quantify with E-factor/PMI/STY/TTN, and integrate in-line product removal and flow. The review ties choices to measurable impacts and gives actionable thresholds (e.g., target product concentration leaving reactor to tame downstream costs).

Higher-order topic map (authors’ framing)

  1. Green metrics & sustainability — E-factor vs PMI (MI), simple vs complete E (incl. solvents/water), link to cost; complement with LCA/eco-efficiency tools (GREENSCOPE, FLASC).

  2. Biocatalyst formats — flowsheets for resting whole cells, isolated enzymes, immobilized enzymes; format dictates flowsheet & recycle.

  3. Media engineering — Water (push ≥50–300 g/L product to cap water removal); organic/biphasic; scCO₂ (31 °C, 7.4 MPa) for nonvolatile, high mass-transfer; ionic liquids (IL/PIL) & DES/NADES (choline-based) with design rules and DS options (e.g., scCO₂ extraction).

  4. Protein & substrate engineering — evolution for activity/selectivity/stability; substrate engineering & promiscuity/chemomimetic leverage.

  5. Biocatalyst engineering — Immobilization methods, CLEAs/CLECs, magnetic formats; reuse & stability vs mass-transfer.

  6. Reactor/ISR/flow — packed beds, flow with in-situ/inline product removal (solvent swap, phase-split), design for high substrate loads and STY.

  7. Cascades (cell-free synthetic biology) — linear/orthogonal with cofactor strategies; bridge to manufacturing.

  8. Bio-based economy — starch & lignocellulose hydrolysis/pretreatment; food/beverage polysaccharide processing.

Key exemplars & metrics (for your tables)

  • Metrics in practice: E-factor correlates with API cost; simple vs complete E clarify solvent/water burdens; PMI = E+1; add EQ weightings and LCA indicators for fuller assessments.

  • Reactor/productivity rules: push outlet product concentration (≈ 50–300 g/L by product value tier) to trim water removal; higher substrate concentration ↓ E-factor (inverse correlation).

  • Solvent swap: concentrate by extracting from water into a lower-bp solvent before isolation (cost & green wins).

  • scCO₂ & IL/DES: lipase in scCO₂ improves rates vs hexane; PILs enable high-ee resolutions with minimal background; DES (ChCl/urea or glycerol) supports CaLB transesterifications amidations with toluene-like conversions; choline/sorbitol DES cut epoxidation E-factor 7.7 vs 16.7 in [BMIM][BF₄].

  • Industrial case (evolution + process): atorvastatin intermediate — evolved KRED/GDH/HHDH lifted TTN, STY (→25–28 g L⁻¹ h⁻¹), suppressed inhibition, and delivered overall E-factor 5.8 (excl. water); enzyme/cofactor waste <1%.

  • API route comparison: 7-ACA enzymatic vs chemical — enzymatic route wins on RME, energy and GHG, though used more water (higher E if water counted).

Limits / gaps (authors’ view)
Solvent/water burdens dominate PMI/E; predictive enzyme–IL compatibility still weak; need standardized reporting of process-relevant metrics; many comparisons confounded by maturity gap between routes.

Outlook
“Sustainable-by-design” = evolve catalysts to fit the process, not vice-versa; combine evolution + immobilization + ISR + flow; broaden task-specific IL/DES and scCO₂ strategies; embed metrics/LCA + economics early to steer toward low-PMI/high-STY routes.

Tags

Biocatalysis Field Topics - From Sheldon & Woodley Review

Source: Sheldon, R.A. & Woodley, J.M. “Role of Biocatalysis in Sustainable Chemistry.” Chemical Reviews 118, 801-838 (2018).

Core Topics Identified

1. Sustainable Chemistry & Green Chemistry Principles

Definition: Framework for environmentally benign chemical processes using the 12 principles of green chemistry. Key Quote: “Green chemistry efficiently utilizes (preferably renewable) raw materials, eliminates waste and avoids the use of toxic and/or hazardous reagents and solvents in the manufacture and application of chemical products.”

2. Environmental Metrics & Life Cycle Assessment

Definition: Quantitative measures for assessing environmental impact of processes. Key Quote: “The E factor, in contrast, is the actual amount of waste produced in the process… Various alternative metrics have also been proposed for measuring the environmental footprints of processes.”

3. Alternative Reaction Media

Definition: Non-conventional solvents for biocatalytic reactions. Key Quote: “There is a growing interest, therefore, in the use of avant garde solvents such as ionic liquids (ILs) and deep eutectic solvents (DESs) for conducting enzymatic transformations.”

4. Ionic Liquids (ILs) for Biocatalysis

Definition: Room temperature molten salts as reaction media for enzymes. Key Quote: “ILs are biocompatible with many enzymes and are eminently suitable as alternative reaction media for biotransformations.”

5. Deep Eutectic Solvents (DESs)

Definition: Eutectic mixtures of hydrogen bond donors and acceptors as green solvents. Key Quote: “DESs are produced by mixing ammonium or phosphonium salts with a hydrogen-bond donor… they are generally nontoxic and biodegradable.”

6. Protein Engineering for Industrial Applications

Definition: Enzyme optimization for commercial processes using directed evolution. Key Quote: “Today, it is eminently feasible to optimize enzymes to fit a predefined optimum process, that is, genuine benign by design.”

7. Substrate Engineering & Enzyme Promiscuity

Definition: Using enzymes with non-natural substrates and reactions. Key Quote: “Substrate engineering can lead to both the optimization of existing biocatalytic reactions and the invention of completely new transformations, which gave rise to the term enzyme promiscuity.”

8. Chemomimetic Biocatalysis

Definition: Creating semi-synthetic metalloenzymes for new reactions. Key Quote: “This chemomimetic biocatalysis strategy, in which a protein scaffold forms the starting point for modification, was successfully used to develop a semisynthetic peroxidase.”

9. Cross-Linked Enzyme Aggregates (CLEAs)

Definition: Carrier-free enzyme immobilization technology. Key Quote: “Cross-linking of these physical aggregates renders them permanently insoluble while maintaining their preorganized superstructure and, hence, their catalytic activity.”

10. Magnetic Enzyme Recovery Systems

Definition: Using magnetic nanoparticles for enzyme separation and reuse. Key Quote: “‘Smart’ ferromagnetic immobilized enzymes that can be easily separated magnetically on an industrial scale using standard commercial equipment.”

11. Reactor Engineering for Biocatalysis

Definition: Specialized reactor designs for enzymatic processes. Key Quote: “Chemical reactions used for commercial production need to be operated in an efficient manner with respect to starting material, catalyst, and equipment.”

12. In Situ Product Removal (ISPR)

Definition: Process intensification through integrated product separation. Key Quote: “One of the major limitations for the implementation of many biocatalytic processes is the low concentration of product leaving the reactor… in situ product removal (ISPR) can afford order-of-magnitude improvements in the process.”

13. Continuous Flow Biocatalysis

Definition: Flow chemistry techniques applied to enzymatic reactions. Key Quote: “The application of continuous-flow techniques can favorably influence the sustainability of manufacturing processes.”

14. Multi-Enzyme Cascade Reactions

Definition: Sequential enzymatic transformations in one-pot processes. Key Quote: “Biocatalytic cascade processes have become a particular focus of attention in recent years, motivated largely by potential environmental and economic benefits.”

15. Combi-CLEAs Technology

Definition: Co-immobilized multiple enzymes for cascade reactions. Key Quote: “The rates of biocatalytic cascades can be substantially increased by simulating the close proximity of the enzymes present in microbial cells… through the coimmobilization of two or more enzymes in a so-called combi-CLEA.”

16. Biobased Economy Applications

Definition: Using biocatalysis for converting renewable biomass to chemicals. Key Quote: “White biotechnology, with its underpinning tools of enzymes and microorganisms, is a key technological driver of this growing biobased economy.”

17. Lignocellulose Bioprocessing

Definition: Enzymatic conversion of cellulosic biomass to fermentable sugars. Key Quote: “The complex, recalcitrant structure of lignocellulose… requires the use of a complex cocktail of cellulolytic and hemicellulolytic enzymes, referred to collectively as cellulase.”

18. Process Integration & Manufacturing

Definition: Incorporating biocatalytic steps into chemical manufacturing. Key Quote: “An essential step in enabling the further implementation of biocatalysis into common industrial practice is its integration into the broader synthesis and manufacturing context.”

Key Industrial Examples

Atorvastatin Process: Three-enzyme cascade (KRED + GDH + HHDH)

  • Quote: “Assessment of this process according to the metrics and 12 principles of green chemistry clearly showed that it is substantially greener than previous processes.”

Sitagliptin Synthesis: Engineered transaminase replacing metal catalysis

  • Quote: “The biocatalytic process not only reduced the total waste and eliminated all transition metals, but increased the overall yield and the productivity by 53%.”

Quantitative Sustainability Metrics

  • E-factor: kg waste per kg product
  • Process Mass Intensity (PMI): Total mass used per mass of product
  • Atom Economy: Theoretical efficiency of atom utilization
  • Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): Cradle-to-grave environmental impact

Future Challenges Highlighted

  • Product Concentration: Need for >50-300 g/L depending on product value
  • Cofactor Recycling: Sustainable regeneration systems required
  • Process Development Speed: Faster methods needed for industrial adoption
  • Multidisciplinary Integration: Bridging chemistry, biology, and engineering
