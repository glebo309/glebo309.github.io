Biocatalysis

Biocatalysis: Definition and Scope

Definition

Biocatalysis refers to the use of natural catalysts, such as enzymes or whole cells, to perform chemical transformations on organic compounds. These biocatalysts are derived from biological systems and can be employed in their native form or after modification through protein engineering techniques. Biocatalysis represents an interdisciplinary field at the intersection of biochemistry, molecular biology, organic chemistry, and chemical engineering.

Core Principles

Biocatalysts operate under the same fundamental principles as traditional chemical catalysts—they accelerate chemical reactions by lowering activation energy barriers without being consumed in the process. However, they differ from conventional catalysts in several critical aspects:

  1. Selectivity: Biocatalysts typically exhibit exceptional chemo-, regio-, and stereoselectivity.
  2. Mild Reaction Conditions: Most biocatalytic processes operate under physiological or near-physiological conditions (aqueous media, ambient temperature, atmospheric pressure, neutral pH).
  3. Catalytic Efficiency: Many enzymes approach theoretical catalytic perfection with kcat/KM values approaching the diffusion limit (108−109/ Ms).
  4. Complexity: Biocatalysts possess complex three-dimensional structures that enable sophisticated molecular recognition and catalytic mechanisms.

Historical Context

The field of biocatalysis has evolved through several distinct phases:

  1. First Wave (Pre-1980s): Utilization of native enzymes in aqueous environments, primarily in the food and pharmaceutical industries.
  2. Second Wave (1980s-1990s): Introduction of protein engineering and recombinant DNA technology, enabling the production of enzymes from any organism in convenient hosts.
  3. Third Wave (2000s-Present): Development of directed evolution methods leading to highly engineered biocatalysts with properties far beyond their natural counterparts.
  4. Fourth Wave (Emerging): Integration of biocatalysis with synthetic biology, machine learning, computational design, and high-throughput methodologies.

Sources of Biocatalysts

Enzymes for biocatalytic applications can be sourced through several avenues:

Commercial Sources

  • Purified enzymes or lyophilized crude cell lysates available directly from chemical vendors
  • Enzyme libraries and kits for screening (oxidoreductases, transferases, hydrolases, etc.)
  • Custom gene synthesis based on known amino acid sequences

Natural Product Biosynthetic Enzymes

  • Mining biosynthetic pathways of specialized metabolites
  • Fe(II) and 2-oxoglutarate-dependent enzymes that catalyze challenging reactions
  • Diels-Alderases, halogenases, methyltransferases, and other biosynthetic enzymes

Metagenomic and In Silico Screening

  • Environmental DNA libraries from diverse sources (marine sponges, soil, microbiomes)
  • Functional-based screening using colorimetric assays or droplet microfluidics
  • Sequence-based screening to find enzymes with similarity to known catalysts
  • In silico mining of genomic databases

Enzyme Engineering and Design

Directed Evolution

  • Creation of genetic diversity through random mutagenesis, site saturation, DNA shuffling
  • High-throughput screening methods (colony-based assays, microfluidics, FACS)
  • Iterative rounds of mutation and selection to improve desired properties
  • Ultra-high-throughput screening of 105−108 variants in picoliter droplets

Computational Enzyme Design

  • Quantum mechanics modeling of active sites and transition states (“theozyme” design)
  • Integration with Rosetta and other protein design software
  • Molecular dynamics simulations to incorporate protein flexibility
  • Identifying distal mutations that impact catalysis through conformational changes
  • Shortest path mapping and ancestral scaffold approaches

Non-Canonical Amino Acids

  • Expanding the genetic code to incorporate novel functionalities
  • Genetically encoded non-natural amino acids for new catalytic mechanisms
  • Engineering aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases for specific non-canonical amino acids
  • Enhanced nucleophilic catalysis through N-methylhistidine incorporation

Cascade Development

Biocatalytic cascades enable multistep transformations without intermediate isolation, offering significant advantages in efficiency and sustainability. Development follows a design-build-optimize cycle:

Design Phase

  • Retrosynthetic analysis using principles of bio-retrosynthesis
  • Computer-aided synthesis planning tools (e.g., RetroBioCat)
  • Linear, orthogonal, or cyclic cascade design
  • Consideration of cofactor requirements and potential incompatibilities

Build Phase

  • Enzyme selection or discovery for each step
  • Implementation options: in vitro (cell-free extracts), in vivo (whole cells), or hybrid approaches
  • Individual validation of cascade steps before combination

Optimize Phase

  • Protein engineering to overcome pathway bottlenecks
  • Process optimization and enzyme immobilization strategies
  • Pathway flux maximization

Industrial Examples

  • Evonik’s whole-cell cascade for diamine production from renewable dicarboxylic acids
  • GSK’s KRED-IRED system for GSK2879552 synthesis via hydrogen-borrowing cascade
  • Merck’s nine-enzyme cascade for HIV inhibitor islatravir synthesis

Industrial Applications and Key Metrics

Metrics for Evaluating Biocatalysts

  • Productivity (g/lh): Amount of product made per reactor volume and time
  • Yield (g/g): Amount of product relative to substrate added
  • Turnover (mol/mol): Amount of product per amount of biocatalyst
  • E factor (g/g): Waste produced relative to product made
  • Process mass intensity: Raw material input relative to product made

Pharmaceutical Industry Applications

  • Sitagliptin manufacture using an engineered transaminase (200 g/l substrate loading)
  • LSD1 inhibitor GSK2879552 synthesis using an evolved imine reductase
  • Molnupiravir synthesis for COVID-19 treatment

Bulk and Fine Chemical Production

  • Acrylamide production using nitrile hydratase (300-500 g/l, 50-100 g/lh productivity)
  • (−)-Ambrox fragrance synthesis using engineered squalene hopene cyclase
  • Enzymatic hydrolysis for flavor compounds (δ-decalactone)

Reproducibility and Data Deposition

Biocatalysis Databases

  • UniProt, BioCatNet, and BRENDA for enzyme sequence and activity data
  • PrenDB for substrate/product information on prenyltransferases
  • CAZy for carbohydrate-active enzymes
  • Need for comprehensive databases capturing reaction outcomes and conditions

Standardization Efforts

  • STRENDA (Standards for Reporting Enzyme Data) guidelines
  • Challenges in data standardization across different scientific communities
  • Smart-laboratory infrastructure for improved data capture

Future Directions

The field of biocatalysis continues to evolve rapidly, with several emerging trends:

  1. Integration with Synthetic Biology: Engineering of entire metabolic pathways for complex transformations
  2. Artificial Metalloenzymes: Combining the selectivity of enzymes with the versatility of metal catalysis
  3. Promiscuous Enzymatic Activity: Exploiting and enhancing secondary activities of natural enzymes
  4. New Reaction Environments: Expanding biocatalysis to extreme conditions and non-conventional media
  5. Sustainable Chemistry: Development of enzymatic alternatives to traditional chemical processes
  6. Machine Learning Integration: Accelerating enzyme discovery and optimization
  7. De Novo Biocatalysts: Creating entirely new enzymes for reactions not found in nature

Challenges and Limitations

Despite significant advances, several challenges remain:

  1. Enzyme Stability: Improving operational stability under industrial conditions
  2. Substrate Scope: Expanding the range of acceptable substrates
  3. Cofactor Regeneration: Developing efficient systems for cofactor recycling
  4. Process Integration: Combining biocatalysis with traditional chemical steps
  5. Economic Viability: Reducing costs to compete with established chemical processes
  6. C-C Bond Formation: Limited enzyme platforms for carbon-carbon bond forming reactions
  7. Scalable Oxidative Enzymes: Challenges in scaling up P450s and other oxidases
  8. Rapid Development: Meeting the accelerated timelines demanded by industry
